## ch2-deemed
type: quote-block

### quote
I am thinking of beauty again, how some things are hunted because we have deemed them beautiful.

### citation
Vuong 191

### setup
Little Dog has walked from a barn into a tobacco field. He follows what he thinks is a wounded animal calling out.

### analysis
Vuong puts the verb first. The reading precedes the harm.

## ch2-verge
type: quote-block

### quote
Because the sunset, like survival, exists only on the verge of its own disappearing. To be gorgeous, you must first be seen, but to be seen allows you to be hunted.

### citation
Vuong 191

### setup
Two pages later, Vuong holds the same field at the moment the sun is going.

### analysis
The same three words sit in both halves. *To be seen.* One verb, one act. *Allows* treats visibility as something the body grants. The body does not.

## ch2-lens
type: lens-bundle

### methodology_quote
relies on stock stereotypes that covertly and overtly link people of color, women of color, and poverty with "bad," while emphasizing that White, middle- to upper-class people embody all that is "good"

### methodology_citation
Solórzano and Yosso 29

### lens_quote
His personal resolve to live openly had the effect of casting him as an object of others' inquiries and intrusions.

### lens_citation
Getsy 117

### analysis
Solórzano and Yosso name the sorting: covertly and overtly, bodies get linked to "bad" or "good." Getsy puts *inquiries* and *intrusions* in the same casting — kinship-asking and violation flow from one act of being seen. The queer lens reads what the sunset names structurally: the same conditions that let a body be loved are the conditions that make it available to harm.
